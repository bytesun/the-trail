# THE TRAIL

## V0.1 — Once Upon a Trail

This is not a screenplay yet.

It is the first rough story version: something that could be told beside a campfire.

---

Richard did not come to the West Coast Trail to prove anything.

At least, that was what he told himself.

He was forty-two, a landscaper in the suburbs outside Vancouver, the kind of man who could look at a cedar hedge and know exactly where to cut. His days were full of lawns, soil, pruning, invoices, and small promises to customers. He was useful. Reliable. Busy.

But ten years earlier, his friend Daniel had pointed at a photo of the West Coast Trail and said, “One day, we should do this.”

Richard had laughed and said, “Sure. One day.”

One day never came.

Daniel died before they walked it together.

So Richard came alone.

Not for adventure. Not for healing. Not for a grand answer.

Just because a promise, even a small one, has weight if you carry it long enough.

On the morning the trip began, Richard stood at Gordon River with three strangers.

The first was Liam, twenty-six, a young firefighter from Calgary. He had new boots, a new pack, a satellite device, a perfect route plan, and the kind of confidence that made everything around him feel like a test. He said he wanted to “crush the trail.” Nobody corrected him.

The second was Margaret, sixty-eight, a retired schoolteacher from Victoria. She packed slowly, moved slowly, and seemed completely unbothered by everyone else’s hurry. She had walked many trails, but she did not talk about them unless asked. On her pack hung a small blue ribbon, faded by weather.

The third was Anna, thirty-two, a German photographer traveling through Canada. Her English was careful and quiet. She carried an old camera and took photos of things other people stepped over: rope knots, wet leaves, rusted signs, boot prints in mud.

They were not a group.

They were only four people waiting for the same boat.

When the ferry crossed Gordon River, nobody said much. The engine was loud. The water was grey. Behind them, the road, cars, phone signal, and ordinary life drifted away.

When they reached the other side, the guide gave the usual warnings: tides, ladders, mud, bears, injury, weather.

Liam smiled like he had been waiting for the challenge.

Margaret adjusted her rain cover.

Anna lifted her camera.

Richard looked back once across the river.

Then they started walking.

The first day was harder than Richard expected. Not impossible. Just honest. The trail did not care that he had prepared. It did not care that he was strong from work. It did not care that he had watched videos, read trip reports, checked tide tables, and packed carefully.

The trail simply existed.

Mud swallowed boots. Roots crossed the path like old bones. Ladders rose higher than they looked in photos. The forest was wet, green, and indifferent.

Liam moved fast. Too fast. He kept looking back as if waiting for others to admire him. Margaret kept a steady pace. Anna stopped often, sometimes in inconvenient places, to photograph something small. Richard stayed somewhere between them, not slow, not fast, still trying to understand what rhythm the trail wanted.

At the first major ladder, Liam climbed ahead and disappeared over the top.

Richard waited below while Margaret began climbing. She was careful but calm. Anna stood behind Richard, looking up.

“You okay?” Richard asked.

Anna nodded, though her face said something else.

“It is higher than YouTube,” she said.

Richard laughed for the first time that day.

“So is everything,” he said.

By evening they reached camp tired and damp. Nobody had enough dry socks. Nobody’s tent went up as smoothly as expected. Liam cursed at a bent peg. Margaret boiled water. Anna photographed steam rising from her cup. Richard sat on a log, looked at the ocean, and realized he had not checked his phone all day.

There was no signal anyway.

That night, Liam asked Richard why he came.

Richard said, “A friend wanted to do it.”

“Where is he?” Liam asked.

Richard looked at the fire for a moment.

“He didn’t make it.”

Liam did not know what to say, so he nodded too quickly and looked away.

Margaret heard but said nothing.

Anna lowered her camera.

The next morning, the tide decided for them.

They had planned to take the beach route, but the water was too high, so they waited. At first Liam hated waiting. He checked the tide chart three times, walked up and down the beach, complained about losing time.

Margaret sat on driftwood and ate oatmeal.

“You don’t worry about schedule?” Liam asked her.

She smiled.

“The ocean has never cared about my schedule.”

Anna took a picture of Margaret sitting beside the sea. Richard collected water and watched waves cover and uncover the rocks like slow breathing.

When the tide finally dropped, they moved together without deciding to. Liam still led, but not as far ahead. Richard helped Anna across slippery stones. Margaret pointed out where the sand was firmer.

By afternoon, rain came.

Not dramatic rain. Not movie rain. Just steady west coast rain that entered sleeves, softened ground, blurred glasses, and made every task take longer.

Liam slipped first.

He went down hard in the mud, one leg twisted under him, pack pulling him sideways. For a second he tried to laugh it off, then his face changed.

Richard reached him first.

“Stay still.”

“I’m fine,” Liam snapped.

“You’re not fine. Stay still.”

Margaret arrived and knelt with surprising ease. She checked his ankle gently.

“I used to coach cross-country,” she said. “Young men always say they’re fine before they can stand.”

Anna stood nearby holding Liam’s poles.

The ankle was not broken, but it was not good.

Their pace changed after that.

Liam hated it. He hated being helped. He hated being passed by people he thought he was stronger than. Most of all, he hated discovering that the trail did not reward pride.

Richard carried some of Liam’s food. Margaret set the pace. Anna walked behind Liam and said nothing, which somehow helped more than encouragement.

That night, in camp, Liam apologized to no one in particular.

“I thought I was ready,” he said.

Margaret stirred her dinner.

“You were ready for the trail you imagined.”

Liam looked at her.

“What does that mean?”

Margaret shrugged.

“Most of us are.”

On the third day, the group became real.

Not through a speech. Not through a decision. Through small things.

Richard waited when Anna stopped to photograph light through cedar branches.

Anna packed away her camera when Liam needed both hands on a muddy descent.

Liam, moving slower now, noticed Margaret’s water bottle was low and filled it without saying anything.

Margaret taught them how to choose a campsite spot that would not become a puddle by midnight.

They began to know the sound of one another’s footsteps.

That evening they camped near a wide beach. The rain stopped just before sunset. For almost fifteen minutes, the whole coast turned gold.

No one spoke.

Anna took one photo, then put the camera down.

Richard noticed.

“No picture?” he asked.

She shook her head.

“Some things are worse after you take them.”

Richard understood exactly what she meant, though he could not have explained it.

Later, around a small fire, Margaret finally spoke of the blue ribbon on her pack.

It had belonged to her husband, Peter. They had walked parts of the island together for thirty years. He had wanted to do the West Coast Trail one more time after retirement, but cancer took that plan away.

“This is not for him,” she said, touching the ribbon. “He is not here.”

Nobody answered.

Then she added, “But I am. So I walk.”

Richard thought of Daniel. Of old promises. Of all the things people say they will do one day.

On the fourth day, they reached a difficult section of ladders and boardwalk.

Liam’s ankle was better but still weak. The old version of him would have pushed hard. The new version stopped before each ladder and checked on Margaret. It annoyed him at first, then didn’t.

Anna became unexpectedly brave on the ladders. She climbed slowly, deliberately, almost gracefully, as if fear were something she could photograph from the inside.

Richard found himself enjoying the work of moving through the trail: lifting, balancing, reaching, waiting. It reminded him of landscaping in a way he had not expected. You could not force a garden. You could only work with what was there.

At a break, Liam asked Richard what he did for work.

“Gardening,” Richard said.

“Like landscaping?”

“Yeah.”

“You like it?”

Richard thought about the question longer than Liam expected.

“I like fixing things people still have to live with.”

Liam laughed.

“That sounds weirdly deep for cutting hedges.”

Richard smiled.

“Most things are deeper if you do them long enough.”

That night Anna shared why she was traveling.

She had left Berlin after a relationship ended. She thought distance would make her feel free, but mostly she had been taking pictures to avoid deciding what came next.

“I like being behind camera,” she said. “Nobody asks you to be in the story.”

Margaret looked at her gently.

“But you are always in the story.”

Anna looked away, embarrassed.

The fifth day was the quietest.

By then they had run out of most easy conversation. Their bodies were tired. Their socks smelled terrible. Everyone had at least one blister. The novelty was gone.

What remained was the trail itself.

Forest. Beach. Mud. Rope. Ladder. Rain. Sun. Wind. Breath.

They walked for long stretches without talking.

Richard began to feel that Daniel was not waiting at the end. Not really. He had known that, of course. But knowing something and accepting it are not the same.

At lunch, Richard took out a small envelope he had carried the entire way.

Inside was a photo of him and Daniel from ten years earlier, younger and laughing on a different mountain. On the back Daniel had written, “One day.”

Richard had never shown anyone.

Now he placed it on a dry piece of driftwood.

Liam leaned over.

“That him?”

Richard nodded.

“He would have liked this,” Anna said.

Richard looked around: Liam eating with muddy hands, Margaret drying socks on a branch, Anna sitting with sand on her pants, the ocean moving in and out as if nothing human had ever mattered very much.

“Yeah,” Richard said. “He would have.”

Near the end of the day, they met another hiker going the opposite direction. He asked the usual question.

“How many days?”

“Five,” Liam said.

“Almost done then.”

The group looked at one another.

Nobody answered right away.

Finally Margaret said, “Maybe.”

The hiker laughed, not understanding, and continued on.

On the last morning, they packed slowly.

Nobody said it was the last day. They all knew.

The trail grew easier, or maybe they had changed enough to stop fighting it. Liam no longer rushed. Anna took fewer photos. Margaret walked in front for a while, blue ribbon moving gently from her pack. Richard stayed behind everyone for a few minutes and watched them walk.

Six days earlier, they had been strangers waiting for a boat.

Now he knew the shape of Liam’s limp, the way Margaret hummed when tired, the way Anna touched her camera before choosing not to use it.

They reached the end without ceremony.

There was no music.

No great speech.

No one collapsed.

No one declared victory.

They simply arrived.

For a while they sat near the beach, packs beside them, looking at the water.

Liam finally said, “I thought I’d feel more… I don’t know. Proud.”

Margaret smiled.

“You’re disappointed?”

“No,” Liam said. “Just different.”

Anna looked at Richard.

“Did you finish what you came for?”

Richard held Daniel’s photo in his hand.

He thought about the promise. About the ferry. The first ladder. Liam in the mud. Margaret’s ribbon. Anna putting down her camera. He thought about Daniel saying “one day,” and all the years Richard had waited for a better time.

Then he put the photo back in the envelope.

“I don’t think I came to finish something,” he said.

“What then?” Liam asked.

Richard looked at the beach, the trees, the three people beside him.

“I think I came to have these six days.”

No one answered.

They did not need to.

Later, before they separated, Anna took one last photo.

This time she asked all of them to stand in it.

Liam complained that he looked terrible. Margaret said that was how people were supposed to look after the West Coast Trail. Richard laughed.

The camera clicked.

Then they went back to their separate lives.

Months later, Richard was working in a neighborhood garden. A customer had branches piled beside the driveway and asked how much it would cost to take them away.

Richard looked at the old woman, then at the branches, then at the small tree she had planted too close to the fence.

“No charge,” he said.

She looked confused.

“Why?”

Richard thought about saying many things. About Daniel. About the trail. About how strangers become companions when the path is hard enough.

Instead, he pointed at the tree.

“It needs more room,” he said. “I can help you move it.”

The woman smiled.

Across the street, a young father struggled to unload soil from a car trunk. Richard noticed him, then looked back at the woman.

“And maybe later,” he said, “you can give him a hand.”

She followed his eyes across the street.

The smallest kind of understanding passed between them.

Richard lifted the branches into his truck.

That evening, at home, he opened a message from Anna. She had sent the photo from the last day.

Four people stood at the end of the West Coast Trail, muddy, tired, and smiling in a way that did not need explaining.

Under the photo, Anna had written:

“We did not finish the trail. We carried it home.”

Richard looked at the words for a long time.

Then he closed the phone, stepped outside, and walked slowly down the street.

Not toward anywhere important.

Just walking.

The road was quiet.

The evening air smelled like cedar.

And for once, he was not late for anything.
