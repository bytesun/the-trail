# THE TRAIL

## Story Version 0.2 — The West Coast Trail Cut

This version rebuilds the film around the real six-day West Coast Trail itinerary.

The goal is no longer to place a story on the West Coast Trail.

The goal is to let the West Coast Trail shape the story.

> The West Coast Trail is not our setting.
> It is our co-writer.

---

## Core Direction

V0.1 found the emotional shape of the film.

V0.2 finds its body.

The film now follows the classic six-day northbound route:

```text
Day 1 — Gordon River → Thrasher Cove
Day 2 — Thrasher Cove → Camper Bay
Day 3 — Camper Bay → Walbran Creek, through Owen Point
Day 4 — Walbran Creek → Cribs Creek
Day 5 — Cribs Creek → Tsusiat Falls
Day 6 — Tsusiat Falls → Pachena Bay
```

Each day is defined by the real character of that section.

- Day 1: ladders, shock, humility
- Day 2: boulders, coastline, respect
- Day 3: tide, Owen Point, timing
- Day 4: endless ladders, mud, endurance
- Day 5: beach, food, joy, Tsusiat Falls
- Day 6: long walk, farewell, return

---

## Three-Act Shape

### Act I — Arrival: Day 1–2

They arrive as strangers.

They think they are going on a hike.

The trail corrects them.

### Act II — Respect: Day 3–4

The trail begins to decide the rhythm.

Tide, mud, ladders, weather and fatigue replace personal plans.

The group forms naturally, not because anyone announces it.

### Act III — Return: Day 5–6

The trail becomes beautiful only after it has humbled them.

The ending is not achievement.

The ending is carrying the trail home.

# Day 1 — Gordon River → Thrasher Cove

## Key Word

Humility.

## Film Question

What is the West Coast Trail?

By the end of Day 1, the answer is simple:

It is not what you imagined.

---

## Opening Image

Black screen.

Ocean.

A ferry engine.

Wind moving through trees we cannot yet see.

Text appears:

```text
Vancouver Island
June
```

The ferry moves across Gordon River.

No music.

Only engine, water, ropes, backpacks shifting, zippers opening and closing.

---

## Gordon River

Richard arrives early, but he is not the first.

Other hikers are already there.

Some are calm.

Some are pretending to be calm.

Some are checking equipment for the last time.

One person adjusts a GoPro.

Another tightens new boots that have clearly never seen mud.

Someone drinks coffee from a metal cup.

Nobody knows anyone yet.

They look at one another in the careful way strangers do before a shared hardship.

Not unfriendly.

Not friendly either.

Just aware.

Across the water, the forest waits.

It looks darker than the videos.

---

## The Crossing

The boat arrives.

People line up.

Packs are lifted.

Poles clatter against the boat floor.

The engine starts.

As the ferry pulls away, the road, cars, and phone signal stay behind.

No one says this out loud.

Everyone feels it.

A guide gives the final warnings:

Tides.

Ladders.

Bears.

Wolves.

Injuries.

Mud.

Weather.

Richard hears the words, but not fully.

He keeps looking at the trees.

He has watched videos. He has packed carefully. He has planned.

But planning and arriving are different things.

---

## First Steps

There is no ceremony.

No dramatic entrance.

The West Coast Trail begins with a boot sinking into mud.

A wet sound.

Someone laughs.

Someone says:

> Well... here we go.

The group moves into the forest.

At first, everyone still carries the rhythm of ordinary life.

They walk too fast.

They check watches.

They compare maps.

They pass one another, then meet again at small pauses.

They are not a team.

They are simply people discovering the weight of their own backpacks.

The forest grows wetter.

Roots cross the trail.

The air smells of cedar, mud and salt.

The trail climbs.

Then climbs more.

---

## The First Ladder

There is no warning.

A turn in the trail.

Then the first great ladder appears.

Almost vertical.

Longer than expected.

Higher than video allowed anyone to feel.

Richard stops.

He looks up.

The camera stays on his face first.

Only then does it reveal the ladder.

Behind him, a younger hiker whispers:

> Holy...

He does not finish the sentence.

No one laughs this time.

Nobody says they are afraid.

But everyone tightens their grip.

The first climb is quiet.

No music.

Only boots on wet wood.

Hands on rope.

Breathing.

A pack scraping against a rail.

Someone below says:

> Take your time.

Nobody knows who said it.

It is the first small kindness of the trail.

---

## At the Top

At the top, nobody celebrates.

They put down packs.

They breathe.

They drink water.

One person pretends to check the view, but really just needs a moment.

Richard looks back.

The forest is below him now.

For the first time, he understands:

This is not a normal hike.

The West Coast Trail has started teaching before anyone has introduced themselves.

---

## Afternoon

The afternoon becomes repetition.

Climb.

Descend.

Mud.

Roots.

More ladders.

More climbing.

More silence.

People who were talkative in the morning now save breath.

The trail does not become easier.

The hikers become quieter.

Richard notices things he would normally miss:

The way gloves darken from wet wood.

The way everyone adjusts shoulder straps after each ladder.

The way a person who rushes ahead still waits at the next hard spot.

Without discussion, the strangers begin to form loose awareness of one another.

Not friendship.

Not yet.

Just awareness.

---

## First Sound of the Ocean

Late afternoon.

Before they see the ocean, they hear it.

A low, constant sound beyond the trees.

At first it is almost mistaken for wind.

Then it grows.

Then the forest opens.

The Pacific appears.

No one says wow.

They are too tired for performance.

They simply stand there.

For a few seconds, everyone forgets the pack.

---

## Thrasher Cove

The descent to Thrasher is steep enough to feel like the trail is making one final joke.

Camp appears slowly:

Driftwood.

Wet sand.

Tents.

Smoke.

People moving stiffly.

The ocean never stops.

Richard finds a place for his tent.

The ground is not ideal.

Nothing is ideal.

He sets up slowly.

By the time dinner starts, his shoulders ache.

His hands smell like rope and cedar.

His legs feel heavier than expected.

This first day has not been long by distance.

But distance is not how the West Coast Trail measures a day.

---

## Evening

There is a small fire.

There is instant food.

There are socks hanging badly from branches.

People speak more gently now.

The young hiker from the ladder comes over with noodles.

> Mind if I sit?

Richard nods.

They sit on driftwood facing the ocean.

For half a minute, no one speaks.

This silence is not awkward.

It is earned.

Finally the younger hiker says:

> I thought it would be easier.

Richard looks at the sea.

He smiles slightly.

> Tomorrow will tell.

The ocean continues.

Tents glow faintly in the dark.

The first day ends not with achievement, but with humility.

Black screen.

Text:

```text
DAY 1
Gordon River → Thrasher Cove
```

---

## Day 1 Director's Note

Day 1 should not feel like plot.

It should feel like arrival.

The drama is not external conflict.

The drama is the moment when every hiker realizes that the West Coast Trail is more real, more vertical, wetter, slower and more demanding than the version they imagined.

Day 1 lowers everyone's voice.
